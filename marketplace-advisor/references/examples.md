# Worked Examples

Three examples grounding the skill's output format. Use these to calibrate
tone, depth, and structure before generating a response.

---

## Example 1: Used Appliance with Age Risk Flag (Stage 2)

### Input

**User:** I found this listing on Kijiji in Saskatoon. Wondering if it's worth
pursuing. I want something that'll last me another 10–15 years if possible.

> **Miele S5 Callisto Canister Vacuum — $85 OBO**
> Runs great, barely used, picked up at an estate sale last year. Some scratches
> on the plastic body but suction is very strong. Original bags included (about
> half a box left). Located in Nutana, pickup only.

---

### Output

**Note:** In practice, Claude would ask for location first if not already known.
Since Saskatoon is established in this example, proceed directly.

---

**Marketplace Report**
**Item:** Miele S5 Callisto Canister Vacuum
**Platform:** Kijiji
**Asking price:** $85 CAD (OBO)
**Assessed:** [date]

---

**Verdict**
The vacuum itself is excellent — the S5 series is genuinely well-regarded — but
the math on your ownership horizon doesn't work. This unit is approximately
15–19 years old, and you've said you want 10–15 more years of use. Pass, or
use it as a short-term bridge while you keep looking.

**Deal Rating**
★★☆☆☆ — Good vacuum, wrong age. The price is fair for what it is, but not
for what you need it to be.

---

**Price Check**
**Asking:** $85
**Fair market range (secondhand):** $60–$120 for working S5-series units
**New price:** Equivalent current Miele (C3 series) $400–$650 CAD
**Assessment:** Fair price for a working S5 in this condition
**Suggested counter-offer:** Not applicable — the issue is age, not price

**Age Risk**
**Estimated age:** 15–19 years (S5 Callisto production run: approximately 2005–2010)
**Ownership horizon match:** No — you want 10–15 more years; this unit has
already used most of its expected service life
**Risk level:** High
**Mechanism:** This is a **spent longevity** situation. The Miele S5 series has
an excellent reputation for durability — units regularly last 15–20 years. But
this one is already 15–19 years old, which means its longevity has largely been
spent. Specific risks at this age: plastic degradation in the body, hinges, and
bag compartment clips (brittleness and snapping on contact); motor brush wear;
and the cord retract mechanism, which is one of the first things to fail on
aging Miele canisters. The estate sale provenance also means usage history
is completely unknown.

**Category Landscape**
No major generational transition in bagged canister vacuums that affects this
decision. Note: if you were considering bagless alternatives, bagged units like
this Miele generally have longer service lives than bagless models — the bag
protects the motor from fine particulate. For your 10–15 year ownership horizon,
bagged is actually the better category choice.

---

**Red Flags**

- Age. 15–19 years old against a 10–15 year ownership horizon is a mismatch
  regardless of how well it runs today
- Estate sale provenance: unknown usage history, unknown who serviced it,
  unknown what it was used on (construction debris? pet hair? commercial use?)
- "Barely used" is a common estate sale description that means the previous
  owner didn't use it much — it doesn't speak to the item's actual age or
  condition of its components
- "Some scratches on the plastic body": plastic scratches on a 15+ year old
  Miele can indicate brittleness beginning; worth inspecting carefully

**Green Flags**

- Miele GN/C3 compatible bags are still widely manufactured and available —
  no consumable supply problem
- Local pickup only, Nutana address: reduces shipping scam risk, suggests
  a real seller with a real item
- Seller's description is specific (model name, bag situation, scratch
  acknowledgment) — not a copy-paste scam listing
- $85 is a fair ask, not a suspiciously low price

**Scam Awareness**
No active scam flags for this listing. Local pickup, specific description,
reasonable price. Standard caution applies: view in person before paying.

---

**Questions to Ask the Seller**

1. Do you know roughly when it was purchased originally?
2. Has it ever been serviced or repaired?
3. What was it primarily used for — carpets, hard floors, both?
4. Would you be willing to demonstrate it running before I pay?

**What to Inspect In Person**

1. Ask to run it for at least 5 minutes before deciding
2. Flex all plastic clips, latches, and the bag compartment door — feel for
   brittleness or cracking
3. Check the cord retract: extend it fully, then press retract. Should snap
   back smoothly and completely
4. Check the hose connection at both ends for play, cracks, or stiffness
5. Listen for any grinding, rattling, or pitch changes during operation
6. Check suction with your hand over the hose end — should feel strong and even
7. Ask to see it pick up a small pile of debris on a hard floor

**Walk-Away Triggers**

- Any plastic component that flexes and cracks, or shows white stress marks
- Cord retract that's sluggish, stops partway, or doesn't fully retract
- Rattling or grinding sounds during operation
- Suction that's inconsistent or noticeably weaker than a new machine

---

**Category Notes**
Miele canisters are genuinely among the most durable vacuums available — their
15–20 year service life reputation is well-earned. But that reputation applies
to a unit at the _start_ of its life, not the end. For your ownership horizon,
target a Miele C3 or S6/S8 series unit from 2015 or later — you'll likely pay
$150–$250 on Kijiji for a well-maintained one, but you'll actually get the 10+
years of use you're looking for. The S5 Callisto at $85 is a reasonable short-term
vacuum if you need something now and plan to replace it in 3–5 years.

## Example 2: Laptop Search — Category Landscape (Stage 1)

### Input

**User:** I'm looking for a used MacBook for university work, everyday tasks,
and some light photo editing. Budget is around $500–$600 Canadian. What should
I be looking for on Marketplace?

---

### Output

This is Stage 1 — no specific listing, category research mode. The Category
Landscape Analysis is the core of this output.

---

**Before I give you a buyer's guide, I want to flag something important about
the MacBook category at your budget.**

There's a significant generational transition in MacBooks that happened between
2020 and 2023 — from Intel processors to Apple Silicon (the M-series chips: M1,
M2, M3). This shift is one of the most meaningful in laptop history, and at your
$500–600 CAD budget, you're right at the fault line where you might end up with
either generation depending on what you pick.

**Why this matters for you specifically:**

- **Battery life:** M1 MacBooks get 15–18 hours of real-world use. Intel MacBooks
  (especially 2017–2019 models) often get 4–7 hours after a few years of battery
  aging. For university work, this difference is significant.
- **Performance:** M1 handles light photo editing in Lightroom noticeably faster
  than comparable Intel models, with no fan noise.
- **Software:** Almost all software is now M1/M2 native. A small number of older
  apps run via Rosetta 2 emulation, transparently and with no real cost — this
  is rarely a practical issue.
- **Longevity:** Apple stopped releasing macOS updates for Intel MacBooks from
  2017 and earlier. 2018–2019 Intel models will likely stop receiving security
  updates within 2–3 years.

**What's on the market at $500–600 CAD right now:**
The secondhand market is currently flooded with Intel MacBooks as people sell
them to fund M-series upgrades. This means good deals exist on Intel hardware —
but the flood exists for a reason.

At your budget, you may be able to find:

- M1 MacBook Air (2020): typically $700–900 CAD used, occasionally $650 in
  good condition — slightly above budget but worth stretching for
- Intel MacBook Air (2019–2020): $450–600 CAD — in budget, still capable,
  but with 2–4 years of useful life remaining before macOS support phases out
- Intel MacBook Pro 13" (2019–2020): $550–700 CAD — more power but heavier

**My recommendation before you start searching:**
Decide whether you're willing to stretch slightly past $600 for an M1, or
whether you're comfortable with an Intel machine that will serve you well for
3–4 years. Both are defensible choices — just make it a deliberate one.

**What to look for when evaluating specific listings:**

_Model year matters more than specs at this range._ A 2019 Intel MacBook Air
with 8GB RAM is more useful to know than "MacBook Air 1.6GHz."

_Battery health:_ On any MacBook, ask the seller to check Settings → System
Report → Power → Cycle Count. Under 500 cycles is healthy; over 800 means
the battery may need replacement soon ($200–250 at an Apple Store).

_Screen:_ Macs are prone to delamination on older screens (a cloudy/sparkly
coating). Check this in a dark room.

_Signs of liquid damage:_ On Intel MacBooks, look for corrosion on the charging
port and keyboard. M1 MacBooks are somewhat more resilient but still worth checking.

_Original charger:_ Not a dealbreaker but original Apple MagSafe or USB-C
chargers are worth something. Third-party chargers can cause charging issues.

**Suggested search terms:** "MacBook Air M1", "MacBook Air 2020", "MacBook Pro
2019 2020". Avoid: anything 2017 or earlier for your use case.

## Example 3: Whimsical Listing (✨ Whimsical Mode)

### Input

**User:** /marketplace-advisor

> **Facebook Marketplace — Greater Ravenmoor Area**
> **Slightly Used Enchanted Mirror — $40 OBO**
> Great condition. Some minor haunting (3–4 apparitions/week, described by
> previous owner as "mostly harmless"). Previous owner is relocating to a
> smaller realm and can't take it. Original enchantment warranty card included
> but it's written in Elvish. No shipping — curses don't travel well. Pickup
> only from a neutral location.

---

### Output

✨

**Marketplace Report**
**Item:** Slightly Used Enchanted Mirror (provenance unspecified)
**Platform:** Facebook Marketplace
**Region:** Greater Ravenmoor Area
**Asking price:** $40 OBO
**Assessed:** [date]

---

**Verdict**
Potentially a fair deal for a mirror with minor haunting, but the listing has
several unresolved disclosure gaps that need answers before pickup. Proceed
with cautious interest.

**Deal Rating**
★★★☆☆ — Priced right for the haunting level claimed, but "mostly harmless"
is doing a lot of work in this listing.

---

**Price Check**
**Asking:** $40
**Fair market range (secondhand):** $35–65 for minor-haunting mirrors in
good condition (Goblin Exchange recent sold listings); documented-provenance
pieces with transferable binding records have cleared $95–140 at Murkmoor
Auctions this season
**New price:** Enchanted mirrors with fresh binding start at $200 from reputable
artificers; $350+ with warranty
**Assessment:** Fair for the claimed haunting level, assuming claims are accurate
**Suggested counter-offer:** $30, citing the Elvish warranty barrier and
undisclosed apparition nature

**Age Risk**
**Estimated age:** Unknown — seller does not specify
**Ownership horizon match:** Potentially fine. Unlike mundane appliances, enchanted
mirrors do not degrade with age if properly maintained; the enchantment may
actually strengthen over decades. However, age of unknown origin items can
indicate accumulated curse accumulation.
**Risk level:** Medium (age unknown, not inherently risky but provenance gap)

**Category Landscape**
The enchanted mirror market has seen significant consolidation since the Third
Realm Accords limited new enchantment licensing. This has depressed new-mirror
prices slightly but driven up secondhand demand for pre-Accords pieces with
transferable enchantments. The RuneCutter currently names the Caldwell & Sons
Clarity Binding as its top residential pick — a reasonable recommendation for
new purchases, though it's worth noting Caldwell is a founding sponsor of the
RuneCutter's annual Enchantment Summit. For secondhand buyers, the Gray Almanac's
longer dataset is more relevant: pre-Accords mirrors with documented Veil Registry
provenance consistently outperform newer Clarity Bindings over a ten-year horizon.
Buyer's position on this listing: decent, but the missing Registry documentation
is doing real damage to the negotiating case.

---

**Red Flags**

- "Mostly harmless" is unquantified. Mostly is doing significant work here.
  What is the nature of the apparitions? Do they interact with occupants? Have
  they ever escalated?
- No Veil Registry reference number provided. A legitimate enchanted mirror
  should have Registry documentation; the Elvish warranty card is not a
  substitute and is unenforceable outside its original jurisdiction. Functionally,
  there is no transferable warranty.
- No mention of what triggered the haunting originally or whether it has ever
  been exorcised and re-acquired
- "Relocating to a smaller realm" is a common motivated-seller story; not
  necessarily false, but worth noting
- Neutral pickup location is appropriate caution for an enchanted item, but
  confirm the seller isn't insisting on a location they have ritual claim to

**Green Flags**

- Pickup only, no shipping: shows awareness that curses don't travel well,
  which suggests a seller with actual experience with enchanted goods
- Physical warranty card exists, even if in Elvish: suggests the mirror has
  some documented history rather than being of completely unknown origin
- $40 with OBO: seller is motivated and not trying to extract maximum value,
  which usually indicates a genuine transaction rather than a scam

**Scam Awareness**
Enchanted mirror scams in this region typically involve: mundane mirrors with
a temporary glamour presenting as enchanted; mirrors bound with malicious rather
than neutral spirits (distinguishable only by True Sight or a qualified cleric's
assessment); and "warranty transfer" scams where the buyer pays extra for a
transfer that has no legal standing. Do not pay separately for the warranty.

---

**Questions to Ask the Seller**

1. What specifically do the apparitions do? Do they speak, gesture, or make
   physical contact with objects?
2. Have they ever behaved in a way that wasn't "mostly harmless"? Under what
   circumstances?
3. Do you know the original binding artificer, or have any documentation
   beyond the Elvish warranty card?
4. Has the mirror ever been exorcised, re-bound, or had any ritual work done?
5. Why a neutral pickup location specifically — is there a reason it can't
   be viewed at the current address?

**What to Inspect In Person**

1. Bring someone with True Sight or a basic Discern Enchantment cantrip if
   possible — you want to see the binding structure, not just the frame
2. Do not look directly into the mirror during the first 10 minutes of
   inspection — allow it to acclimate to your presence
3. Count the apparitions that appear during a 20-minute viewing window;
   "3–4 per week" should mean zero or one during this window, not a crowd
4. Check the frame for signs of previous binding work (scorch marks,
   ritual inscription overlays, mismatched gilding sections)
5. Ask the seller to stand next to you during the inspection — a seller
   unwilling to be near their own mirror during your viewing is a red flag

**Walk-Away Triggers**

- Any apparition that makes direct eye contact, speaks your name, or gestures
  toward an exit
- More than one apparition visible during a 20-minute inspection window
- Frame that shows signs of previous binding removal and re-application
- Seller who won't stay for the inspection or rushes the viewing
- Any sensation of temporal displacement during the inspection

---

**Category Notes**
Minor-haunting mirrors are a legitimate and reasonably common category on the
Ravenmoor secondhand market — many households have one without incident for
decades. The key variable is the nature of the haunting: residual hauntings
(images, sounds, occasional apparitions tied to past events) are benign;
interactive hauntings (entities that respond to your presence, learn your
name, or seem to have agency) are a different matter entirely and require
professional binding review before purchase. The Gray Almanac's last three
editions have flagged a pattern of interactive hauntings being misrepresented
as residual in private sales; this listing hasn't clarified which type this
is. That's the conversation to have before you commit.
